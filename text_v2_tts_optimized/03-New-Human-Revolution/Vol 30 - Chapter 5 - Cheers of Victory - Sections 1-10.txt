# Volume 30: Chapter 5, Cheers of Victory 1–10

## Cheers of Victory 1

The passage opens with reflection on youth as protagonists of the future, emphasizing their role in shaping society through their aspirations and actions. Shin'ichi Yamamoto, returning from international travels, resolved to focus on developing young people within the Soka Gakkai.

On July 10, 1981, a youth division general meeting commemorated the 30th anniversary of the young men's and women's divisions in Osaka. Yamamoto sent a congratulatory poem expressing hopes for youth leadership, incorporating a Confucian reference to maturity at age thirty.

## Cheers of Victory 2

Yamamoto's message called for global youth unity in advancing peace and kosen-rufu. Simultaneously, approximately one thousand South American members gathered in Foz do Iguaçu, Brazil, for the first regional young men's division general meeting, with participants traveling from across the continent.

## Cheers of Victory 3

President Kiyoshi Jujo died suddenly of a heart attack on July 18, 1981, at age 58. He had served as president since 1979 during turbulent conflicts with the priesthood. Jujo and Yamamoto had worked together for decades, sharing struggles for kosen-rufu and maintaining the mentor-disciple relationship Yamamoto considered essential for organizational continuity.

## Cheers of Victory 4

Yamamoto visited Jujo's home to offer condolences, encouraging his widow to continue his legacy. That afternoon, Vice President Eisuke Akizuki was unanimously appointed as the fifth Soka Gakkai president. Yamamoto believed Akizuki's calm leadership would guide the organization forward while maintaining harmony with changing times.

## Cheers of Victory 5

Formal memorial services occurred July 23-24 at the Tokyo Toda Memorial Auditorium. Yamamoto maintained his demanding schedule, meeting with former Secretary of State Henry Kissinger on July 25 and attending various leadership meetings and regional events through August.

## Cheers of Victory 6

Yamamoto would meet with United Nations Under-Secretary-General Yasushi Akashi eighteen times. These collaborations produced exhibitions on nuclear threats, war and peace, and human rights. In 1992, when Akashi directed UN operations in Cambodia, Soka Gakkai youth collected over 280,000 radios for the nation's post-civil war elections.

The Second SGI General Meeting in Honolulu drew 7,500 international representatives. Commemorative services marked the 700th anniversary of Nichiren Daishonin's passing in October.

## Cheers of Victory 7

Internal priesthood conflicts intensified as the Shoshin-kai group faced expulsion from Nichiren Shoshu. Over 180 priests were eventually removed. Despite harassment, Soka Gakkai members remained committed to kosen-rufu, viewing persecution as validation of their mission.

## Cheers of Victory 8

On October 31, Yamamoto delivered a university lecture titled "Thoughts on History and Historical Figures: Living amid Persecution," discussing eminent figures including Sugawara Michizane, Rai San'yo, Mahatma Gandhi, and Victor Hugo who persevered through adversity. He characterized "persecution is a badge of honor for a Buddhist."

## Cheers of Victory 9

After November 8 activities in the Kansai region, Yamamoto traveled to Tokushima in Shikoku to celebrate completion of the Tokushima Auditorium. Despite uncertainty about his attendance, members prepared eagerly, having endured priest-related hardships while maintaining faith.

## Cheers of Victory 10

Yamamoto arrived unexpectedly on November 9, declaring "I'm here at last! I came to keep my promise to you!" He offered encouragement through prayer, guidance, and piano performances, including "The Three Martyrs of Atsuhara."

The Tokushima location connected historically to Beethoven's Ninth Symphony, which premiered in Asia at nearby Naruto during World War I. German POWs at Bando camp exchanged cultural knowledge with locals, exemplifying how "open heart and mind is the most essential qualification for a world citizen."

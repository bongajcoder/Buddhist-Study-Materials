# Volume 30: Chapter 6, Vow 121–130

## Vow 121

This section emphasizes that humanitarian work requires cooperation across religious boundaries. The text explains that while Nichiren Daishonin criticized certain Buddhist schools, he did so to clarify which teachings aligned with Shakyamuni's true intent, not from intolerance. His ultimate wish was to guide even those who persecuted him toward enlightenment, exemplifying genuine Buddhist compassion.

## Vow 122

People of faith should maintain confidence in their beliefs while remaining humble and open to learning. The passage defines the SGI's mission as building a world free from war through interfaith cooperation and "humanitarian competition"—inspiring each other toward greater contributions.

In 1996, Shin'ichi Yamamoto traveled extensively, receiving an honorary degree from the University of Denver and delivering lectures at Columbia University about global citizenship, defining it as wisdom recognizing life's equality, courage respecting differences, and compassion for others.

## Vow 123

Following the Cold War's end, Cuba faced isolation and economic hardship. Despite U.S. sanctions, Shin'ichi resolved to visit Cuba to promote educational and cultural exchange. He met with former Secretary of State Henry Kissinger, discussing how "firm conviction and vision" could "build a bridge for peace."

## Vow 124

Shin'ichi arrived in Havana on June 24, 1996. He received Cuba's Order of Felix Varela and an honorary doctorate from the University of Havana. During his commemorative lecture, a sudden thunderstorm occurred; he reframed it as "the music of the heavens" encouraging humanity's progress toward peace.

## Vow 125

His lecture proposed three bridges for civilizations of hope: restoring life's wholeness through poetic spirit, bringing people together through empathy, and building futures through education. He met President Fidel Castro for 90 minutes, discussing leadership, education, and dialogue's importance for 21st-century peace.

## Vow 126

Following Shin'ichi's visit, Cuba increased cultural exchanges with Japan. SGI-Cuba gained official religious recognition in 2007, and U.S.-Cuba diplomatic relations eventually restored in 2015.

On June 26, Shin'ichi visited Costa Rica, a nation with constitutional military abolition. He presented members with poetry and participated in opening Latin America's first "Nuclear Arms—Threat to Our World" exhibition, emphasizing that "children are symbols of thriving life" and sources of hope.

## Vow 127

Between 1997 and 2000, Shin'ichi made multiple trips throughout Asia—Hong Kong, China, India, the Philippines, and South Korea—traveling almost continuously.

## Vow 128

In Singapore, President S. R. Nathan praised the Soka youth's performances, noting their discipline and voluntary participation embodied Buddhist teachings. Shin'ichi received an honorary doctorate from the University of Sydney at a Singapore graduation ceremony.

## Vow 129

Chancellor Leonie Kramer warmly presented diplomas to graduates while offering encouraging advice. Shin'ichi honored Tsunesaburo Makiguchi's legacy, highlighting his vision of global interdependence and advocating "soft power" of culture and spirituality over military dominance.

## Vow 130

Shin'ichi visited Singapore Soka Kindergarten, personally greeting each child. He observed their drawings and performed Japanese songs together. Later, he attended an SGI commemorative meeting where he emphasized maintaining faith in the Gohonzon while chanting, sharing "all your joys and sorrows" as with caring parents.
